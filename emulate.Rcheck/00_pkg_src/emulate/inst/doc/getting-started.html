Getting Started with emulate: Target Trial Emulation in R

Timothy P. Copeland

2026-03-10

Introduction

This vignette walks through a complete target trial emulation analysis using the emulate package. We will analyze a bundled example dataset and produce two analyses:

  1. Intention-to-Treat (ITT): What is the effect of being assigned to treatment, regardless of actual adherence?
  2. Per-Protocol (PP): What is the effect of adhering to the assigned treatment strategy?

By the end, you will understand every step of the emulate pipeline and be able to adapt it to your own data.

Step 0: Define the Protocol

Before touching the data, we should define the target trial we are emulating. The emulate_protocol() function creates the 7-component protocol table from Hernan and Robins:

protocol <- emulate_protocol(
  eligibility    = "Adults meeting clinical criteria, not currently on treatment,
                    no prior outcome event",
  treatment      = "Strategy A: Initiate treatment at the start of each eligible
                    period. Strategy B: Do not initiate treatment.",
  assignment     = "Patients are assigned to treatment strategies at each period
                    when they meet eligibility criteria.",
  followup_start = "Start of the period when eligibility is first met for each
                    emulated trial.",
  outcome        = "First occurrence of the binary outcome event during follow-up.
                    Maximum follow-up: 8 periods.",
  causal_contrast = "Intention-to-treat effect and per-protocol effect of
                     treatment initiation vs. no treatment.",
  analysis       = "Sequential trial emulation with pooled logistic regression.
                    PP analysis uses stabilized IPTW with truncation at 1st/99th
                    percentiles. G-formula marginal predictions with 100 MC
                    samples for confidence intervals.",
  format = "display"
)

This step produces no statistical output but forces you to articulate the study design. It can also be exported to Excel or LaTeX for inclusion in manuscripts.

Analysis 1: Intention-to-Treat

The ITT analysis estimates the effect of treatment assignment, ignoring whether patients actually adhere. This is the simplest and most robust analysis.

This is the core of the sequential trials method. For ITT, each eligible individual at each trial period is enrolled and followed for up to 8 periods. No cloning or artificial censoring occurs under ITT – individuals keep their observed treatment and are followed as-is.

The expansion ratio tells you how much the dataset grew. The expanded dataset contains one row per individual per trial per follow-up period.

Interpretation

Look at the treatment arm coefficient (the odds ratio for _emulate_arm). An OR < 1 indicates treatment is protective; an OR > 1 indicates treatment is harmful. The confidence interval and p-value tell you about statistical significance.

The cumulative incidence predictions show the absolute risk over time in each arm. The risk difference at the final time point gives you the absolute treatment effect.

Under PP, each eligible individual is cloned into two copies: one assigned to the treatment arm (arm = 1) and one assigned to the control arm (arm = 0). Each clone is then followed forward and artificially censored when their observed treatment deviates from the assigned strategy:

  • Treatment arm clone: censored when the individual stops treatment
  • Control arm clone: censored when the individual starts treatment

This is the “clone-censor-weight” framework. The expansion ratio is roughly twice that of ITT because of the cloning.

Step 4: Compute inverse probability weights

pp <- emulate_weight(
  pp,
  switch_d_cov = c("catvarA", "catvarB", "catvarC",
                    "nvarA", "nvarB", "nvarC"),
  switch_n_cov = c("catvarA", "catvarB"),
  truncate     = c(1, 99),
  stabilized   = TRUE
)

#> --------------------------------------------------

This is the key step that distinguishes PP from ITT. The weight models estimate the probability of treatment switching and create stabilized inverse probability weights to correct for the informative censoring introduced by the cloning.

The denominator model includes all time-varying covariates – it models the probability of the observed treatment given the full history. The numerator model includes only baseline covariates for stabilization.

The weight summary shows mean, SD, min, max, and effective sample size (ESS). For well-behaved weights: - Mean should be close to 1 (for stabilized weights) - SD should not be too large - ESS should be a reasonable fraction of the sample size

Step 8: Diagnose weight quality

  pp,
  balance_covariates = c("catvarA", "catvarB", "catvarC",

Interpretation

Compare the PP treatment effect to the ITT effect. The PP effect is often larger in magnitude because it estimates the effect under full adherence rather than the diluted intent-to-treat effect. However, the PP estimate relies on the correctness of the weight model – if the weight model is misspecified, the PP estimate may be biased.

Comparing ITT and PP

cat("ITT treatment log-OR:", round(itt$model$b_treat, 4), "\n")
#> ITT treatment log-OR: -0.1915
cat("PP  treatment log-OR:", round(pp$model$b_treat, 4), "\n")
#> PP  treatment log-OR: -0.2232
cat("\n")
cat("ITT treatment OR:", round(exp(itt$model$b_treat), 4), "\n")
#> ITT treatment OR: 0.8257
cat("PP  treatment OR:", round(exp(pp$model$b_treat), 4), "\n")
#> PP  treatment OR: 0.8

Key points for comparison:

  • The ITT estimate is robust to model misspecification for treatment switching but is diluted by non-adherence. It answers: “What happens when we assign patients to treatment?”

  • The PP estimate attempts to estimate the effect under full adherence but depends on correct weight modeling. It answers: “What would happen if everyone followed the protocol?”

  • If the ITT and PP estimates are similar, treatment switching is either rare or does not substantially bias the ITT estimate.

  • If the PP estimate is substantially larger in magnitude, non-adherence is attenuating the ITT effect.

  • If the PP estimate is in the opposite direction from ITT, this is a red flag and may indicate weight model misspecification.

Sensitivity analyses

Robust analyses should assess sensitivity to key modeling choices:

  1. Truncation levels: Try c(1, 99), c(5, 95), and c(10, 90) to see how weight trimming affects estimates.

  2. Time specifications: Compare "linear", "quadratic", "cubic", and "ns(3)" for both follow-up and trial period.

  3. Follow-up length: Vary maxfollowup to see how the estimate changes with longer or shorter follow-up.

  4. Grace periods: If treatment switching is gradual, a grace period of 1-3 periods may be more realistic than strict censoring at the first deviation.

# Grace period analysis
pp_grace <- emulate_expand(pp, maxfollowup = 8, grace = 2)

As-Treated analysis

For absorbing treatments (once started, never stopped), the As-Treated estimand may be appropriate:

at <- emulate_prepare(trial_data, ..., estimand = "AT")
at <- emulate_expand(at, maxfollowup = 8)
at <- emulate_weight(at, switch_d_cov = c("x1", "x2"), truncate = c(1, 99))
at <- emulate_fit(at, outcome_cov = c("x1", "x2"))

For absorbing treatment, AT and PP produce identical results.
